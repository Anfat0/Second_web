Lab Experiments

During my time at HHC Medical ApS, I was responsible for setting up and managing experiments in collaboration with scientists. The company focused on developing wireless electroporation technology, which involved the use of coil-based applicators. In these experiments, I oversaw the operation of the high-voltage generator, seen on the right of the picture, and adjusted its waveform characteristics as needed. To the left, you can see the oscilloscope used to monitor high-voltage and high-current waveforms during testing. The oscilloscope was connected using HV probes and a Rogowski sensor. At the bottom center of the setup, you’ll find the cell well where the targeted cells were treated. This portable system was also utilized in animal experiments with rodents and pigs.

Medical Applicators

The applicator shown in the picture was built using 3D-printed parts designed at the dry lab of the BioInnovation Institute (BII). It also includes an electrical component, with a custom-built coil designed specifically for each experiment. The coil is sealed inside the applicator to allow deionized water to flow through for cooling purposes. This applicator is connected to both the high-voltage generator and a hydraulic circuit, and it was successfully used in animal experiments on pigs.

Hydraulic Circuit Control

The hydraulic circuit is controlled by an Arduino Uno, programmed in C to manage the driver that activates the pump. The pump connects the applicator to a 5-liter bottle of deionized water, maintained at 2-3°C. The constant flow of cold water ensures that the applicator remains cool, preventing any temperature rise that could lead to a short circuit in the coil, thus ensuring safety for the operator. To guarantee safe operation, the applicators undergo multiple dry runs, during which coil temperature, current, and voltage are closely monitored.
